Foundation

Luxury is a Feeling,
Not a Formula

Luxury in digital design is not achieved through ornament — it's achieved through restraint, precision, and intention. The visitor should feel attended to, not impressed upon.

01
Restraint
Less on the page means more impact per element. Every section earns its place — nothing is there by default.
02
Precision
Alignment, spacing, and type choices are deliberate — no defaults, no shortcuts, no "good enough."
03
Intention
Every visual decision serves the brand narrative. Decoration for its own sake erodes trust, not builds it.

Typography

Type Sets the Tone
Before Words Do

Serif for Display
Serif fonts carry the weight of legacy and authority. Use for headlines, pull quotes, and hero moments.
The Standard
is Excellence
Sans for Body
Light-weight sans keeps body copy clean. The contrast between a rich display serif and a restrained body sans is itself a luxury signal.
Line-height — 1.65–1.8 for body copy
Tracking — light positive for caps & labels
Weight — 300 body / 600+ display
Hierarchy is Non-Negotiable
A luxury site has 3 distinct typographic levels. Blur the hierarchy — same size, same weight everywhere — and the layout collapses into visual noise.
Avoid Decorative Overload
Script fonts, three mixed typefaces, and excessive italic use dilute the premium feel. One great serif + one clean sans outperforms a three-font salad every time.
04 / 16
Spacing
"Luxury is communicated
by what is left out."

White space is not empty — it's active. It gives each element room to breathe, weight, and be noticed. A cramped layout signals budget. Generous space signals premium.

Generous
96–120px section padding · 1.65+ line-height · Content given room to assert itself
Cramped
40px section padding · 1.3 line-height · Everything pressing against everything else
05 / 16
Core Principle

The Less-Is-More
Imperative

A luxury site whispers — it never shouts. Every element you add creates visual competition. Every element you remove clarifies hierarchy.

01
One focal point per section
CTAs, headlines, and feature lists should not compete simultaneously for attention. Lead with one clear dominant element.
02
Decoration must earn its place
Thin rules, subtle gradients, and typographic accents are fine — only when they serve structure, not spectacle.
03
Trust the content
Luxury real estate clients sell themselves. Great photography + clean type + space almost always beats heavy UI treatments.
06 / 16
DOCuration

Edit
Ruthlessly

A premium site should feel curated, not comprehensive. Fewer sections done well beats more sections done adequately.

—
Does this section earn its scroll?
If removing a section wouldn't hurt the client's story or conversion path, it shouldn't be there.
—
Quality over quantity, always
3 well-designed sections beats 8 rushed ones. The visitor leaves with a clearer impression when there are fewer, stronger moments.
—
Remove, then justify adding back
When in doubt, take it out. The burden of proof is on every element — not on its removal.
07 / 16
DOConsistency

Maintain Visual
Consistency Page to Page

Same spacing rhythm. Same color usage. Same image treatment. The site should feel like one cohesive experience — not a collection of different templates.

S
Spacing
Section gaps, card padding, and type spacing follow the same scale site-wide. They never drift by section or by designer.
C
Color
Accent colors appear in the same role everywhere. A gold CTA on one page shouldn't become a green CTA on another.
I
Imagery
Same aspect ratios, same crop style, same tonal range. Mixing cinematic dark crops with bright casual shots breaks the narrative.
08 / 16
DONarrative

Consider the Scroll
as a Narrative

Each section should feel intentional and lead the visitor toward a CTA or next step. A luxury site tells a story — it doesn't just list features.

01
Establish → Engage → Convert
Hero introduces the brand. Middle sections build trust. Closing moments drive action. This arc should be visible in the page structure.
02
Each section creates a question the next answers
"Who is this agent?" → "Here are their listings" → "Here's what clients say" → "Here's how to reach them."
03
Pacing matters
Alternate dense and airy sections. Back-to-back content-heavy sections create fatigue. Space them with lighter visual moments.
09 / 16
DON'TOver-Decoration

Don't Over-Decorate

Excessive gradients, drop shadows, borders, and ornamental elements undermine the clean, modern luxury LP is known for. Every decoration you add is a signal clients associate with "template."

Avoid
Multiple overlapping drop shadows
Heavy gradient overlays on images
Decorative border lines everywhere
Glowing accent boxes and halos
Patterned or textured section backgrounds
Instead
Subtle single-direction light source
Minimal, optional image vignette only
Spacing as the primary separator
One hairline accent rule, sparingly used
Solid, confident background color blocks
10 / 16
DON'TClient Trust

No Placeholder Content
in Any Live Section

Lorem ipsum, placeholder headshots, or "Coming Soon" text should never reach client preview. It signals unfinished work and erodes the trust you've built.

—
Use real content or leave the section out
A placeholder is worse than no section at all. If content isn't ready, remove the section entirely until it is.
—
Placeholder headshots are a hard stop
A gray silhouette where the agent's face should be communicates neglect. It's the single most damaging trust signal on a real estate site.
—
"Coming Soon" = incomplete product
If a feature isn't ready, don't tease it. Show what's done beautifully rather than promising what isn't finished.
11 / 16
Imagery

Images Do
the Selling

On a luxury real estate site, photography is the primary product presentation. Image quality is the first proxy for agent quality.

Resolution
Never use compressed or pixelated images. At retina density, low-res photos are immediately visible and signal low budget.
Tonal Consistency
All images on a page share a tonal family — warm, cool, or neutral. Mixed color temperatures make the layout feel cobbled together.
Aspect Ratio Discipline
16:9 for hero, 4:3 for cards, 1:1 for profiles. Consistent crop ratios create rhythm. Mismatched ratios create chaos.
Subject Matter
Exterior shots in golden hour. Interiors with natural light. Never dark, cluttered, or dimly-lit rooms as hero images.
Agent Headshots
Professional, consistent background, tonal match to site palette. Informal selfies undermine the premium positioning of the entire brand.
Load Performance
Use WebP where possible. Lazy-load below-fold images. A luxury site that loads slowly is no longer luxury.
12 / 16
Motion

Move Slowly,
Move With Purpose

Luxury motion is deliberate — not energetic. Slow fades, gentle reveals, and eased transitions reinforce a sense of calm confidence.

✓
Slow fade-up reveals (600–900ms)
Gradual entrances feel considered and polished. Quick pops feel cheap and template-y.
✓
Ease-out cubic-bezier curves
Real-world physics: things ease into rest. Never use linear or bounce — they read as unrefined.
✗
Avoid: fast parallax, spinning loaders, text scrambles
These signal "tech startup" or "agency portfolio" — not luxury real estate authority.
✗
Avoid: hover animation on every element
Selective motion creates emphasis. When everything moves, nothing is special.
13 / 16
Craft

Luxury Lives
in the Details

No single detail defines a luxury site — it's the accumulation of many small-right decisions that separate LP sites from the rest of the market.

Consistent Interaction Radius
All buttons, inputs, and cards use the same border-radius. Mixing pill buttons with sharp-corner cards signals inconsistency.
Footer Typography
Even the footer gets the same typographic care as the hero. Rushed footers are read as the last impression — and they stick.
Deliberate Hover States
Every interactive element needs an on-brand hover state. Default browser blue underlines are not part of any luxury brand system.
Styled Form Inputs
Contact forms are often the conversion moment. An un-styled HTML input on a luxury site is a broken experience — style every field.

Review

Before You Send
the Preview Link

  • Does each section earn its place on the page?
  • Is spacing consistent across all sections?
  • Are all placeholder images replaced with real photos?
  • Is there any Lorem ipsum text anywhere?
  • Do all pages feel like one cohesive experience?
  • Are all CTAs visible and intentionally placed?
  • Is the scroll a coherent narrative with a clear arc?
  • Are decorative elements supporting — not competing?
  • Do all images share tonal and aspect-ratio consistency?
  • Is this site something LP would be proud to pitch?
